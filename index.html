From Arrival to Assimilation:

Immigration and the American Dream

In 2022, there were 46 million people living in the United States who had moved there from somewhere else. Each dot here represents 10,000 people.

In this project, we will focus on one small subset of immigrants: those who moved from China to the San Francisco Bay Area.

We follow these immigrants in the Bay Area, as well as later generations of immigrants who were born in the United States to immigrant parents, or even native-born parents.

Education

To understand education, we can compare the highest level of education achieved. For a fair comparison, we limit our data to adults who are not in school.

First, here is the distribution for first-generation immigrants. The plurality of first-generation immigrants have a post-secondary degree (i.e., they've graduated with an Associate's or Bachelor's degree).

Second generation immigrants have higher educational attainment: they all have receive at minimum primary education, and most at least graduate high school.

In later generations, we see that education attainment starts to flatten. A greater proportion of this generation have at least a primary or secondary education, but the number of those with college degrees decrease.

Comparing to the Bay Area population averages, we find that, although second generation immigrants have higher educational attainment, later generations actually fall below the general population.
