Healthcare


Jessica has been injured in a car accident. After suffering with neck and back pain, she decides to go to the closest hospital’s Emergency Room for treatment. However, the United States is well-known for having a very high cost of care, which can be life-altering for residents who are underinsured or uninsured. In the last part of our analysis, let’s take a look at healthcare coverage across generations to see how they fare compared to Elizabeth or Daniel.



Healthcare coverage is measured by self-reporting. Coverage is defined as the household being covered by any kind of healthcare insurance, including private insurance provided by an employer, medicare, medicaid, or US military insurance.


Immigrants across the board are more likely to have healthcare coverage than the general population. Vice versa, they are also less likely to be uninsured than the general population. Third-gen is the best covered when compared to all other generations, though the margin is small.






Conclusion


The data shows us that when it comes the American Dream, it is not equally applied across all generations of immigrants. As a family reaches assimilation, the American Dream fades from reach. While 1st generation strives for achievement and 2nd generation outperforms their parents, 3rd-gen and on struggle, lagging behind both previous generations of their family and population average.





Glossary


  • The American Dream: For the scope of this project, we have defined the American Dream as a sum of four component parts: education, income, class/occupation, and healthcare coverage. Those who have “achieved” the American Dream and have been most successful under it have access to high levels of education and income, are covered adequately by their healthcare providers, and are able to attain high class status.

  • Generations: In this project we define four main categories of immigrant generation: 1, 1.5, 2, and 3+. The first generation are the original immigrants to the United States. 2nd-gen consists of their children whether native-born or born outside the US. The 3rd generation is composed of the grandchildren of the 1st generation, born within the US and any subsequent children born of the 2nd generation.

  • Bay Area: Within the scope of this project we are defining the Bay Areas as the following counties: Alameda, Marin, San Francisco, Santa Clara, San Mateo, Sonoma, Solano, Napa, and Contra Costa.


Education


  • Primary: Elementary and Middle School
  • Secondary: High school
  • Post-Secondary: Associate’s Degree, Bachelor’s Degree
  • Post-graduate: Professional Degree, Doctorate


Occupation


  • Blue-Collar: Working-class professions that involve a greater degree of physically-taxing or manual labor (e.g. farmers, mechanics, power plant operators, and electricians)
  • White-Collar: Industry worker, characterized by working inside an office
  • Class Mobility: The ability to change your socio-economic situation, either in relation to parents (intergenerational mobility) or throughout your lifetime (intra-generational mobility).


Income


To define income classes, we follow the Pew Research Center’s classifications of low income, middle income, and upper income for an average family of 3.


  • Low Income: Less than $52,200 combined household income
  • Middle Income: $52,200 - $156,600
  • Upper Income: More than $156,600


Healthcare


  • Underinsured: While an underinsured person may have healthcare coverage, the sum they are insured for or the policy they hold is inadequate to cover their needs.
